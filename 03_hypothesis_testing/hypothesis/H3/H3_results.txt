H3 tests whether a non-linear relationship exists between earthquake impact and economic indicators that ensemble methods can capture better than linear ones. The target, recovery_years (years until GDP returned to its pre-event 3-year baseline), was reframed from continuous regression into a 3-class ordinal classification — Fast (1 year), Medium (2–3 years), Slow (4–5 years) — to handle its bounded, heavily skewed, right-censored distribution. An initial run had silently inflated R² because gdp_drop and recovery_years are derived from the same GDP-growth time series in the data-preparation step, constituting target leakage; the corrected experiment removes gdp_drop and re-trains on n = 608 events with complete pre-event economic features. Logistic Regression and a tuned Random Forest (n_estimators=400, min_samples_leaf=2, class_weight=balanced) were compared on identical stratified 5-fold cross-validation splits, with a trivial majority-class baseline (accuracy 0.54, macro-F1 0.23) included as a sanity floor.

Random Forest reached 76.0 % accuracy and 69.3 % macro-F1, beating Logistic Regression (68.6 % and 48.8 %) by +7.4 and +20.6 percentage points, and a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test on per-fold F1 confirms the gap is statistically significant (p = 0.031). Both models clear the trivial baseline by a wide margin, so the signal is real rather than an artefact of class imbalance. Feature importances show the dominant predictors are the country's pre-event growth trend (34 %), unemployment (22 %) and GDP per capita (19 %), with focal depth (11 %) and magnitude (9 %) trailing — a striking reversal of intuition: recovery time depends more on the kind of economy the earthquake hits than on how strong the earthquake is. H3 is therefore supported: meaningful non-linear structure links earthquake impact to economic indicators, and ensemble learning captures it where linear methods cannot.
